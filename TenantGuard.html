Statutory Legal Library

Plain-English explanations of critical United Kingdom housing acts used to defend renters.

Repairs / Safety Housing Act 1985 / 1988

Section 11, Landlord and Tenant Act 1985

This is the most powerful law for securing repairs. It establishes that the landlord holds the statutory obligation to keep in repair the structure and exterior of the dwelling-house (including drains, gutters, and external pipes) and keep in repair and proper working order installations for the supply of water, gas, electricity, and sanitation.

Key Ruling: Landlords cannot shift repair costs to tenants or contract out of Section 11 obligations, regardless of tenancy terms.
Eviction Security Housing Act 1988

Section 21 & Section 8 Evictions

Under modern law, landlords must use specific statutory procedures to evict tenants. A Section 21 "no-fault" eviction must follow strict guidelines: the landlord must provide 2 months' notice, have protected the security deposit in an approved scheme, and have provided the tenant with gas safety certificates and EPC ratings before tenancy began.

Key Ruling: If your landlord hasn't given you a copy of the "How to Rent" booklet and a gas safety check, any Section 21 notice is legally invalid.
Rent Control Housing Act 1988

Section 13, Assured Periodic Tenancies

Landlords cannot raise your rent arbitrarily or via standard text messages during a periodic tenancy. They must serve a formal "Landlord's notice proposing a new rent" using standard **Form 4**. If you object to the proposed rent, you have a statutory right to refer the increase to a First-tier Tribunal before the start date.

Key Ruling: Rent increases must match general local market valuations. If the hike is excessive, Tribunals will force it back down to market rates.
Improvements Housing Act 1985

Section 97, Secure Tenant Alterations

Secure tenants (such as those in council housing or housing associations) have a legal right to carry out refurbishments or improvements. However, under Section 97, they **must** obtain the landlord's written consent first. The landlord cannot withhold consent unreasonably, but verbal consent holds no weight in dispute proceedings.

Key Ruling: If a council representative gives verbal permission on the phone, the tenant bears the burden of proof. Always obtain retrospective or initial written agreements.
